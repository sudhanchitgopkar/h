**Capital Markets** Sudhan Chitgopkar, Harvard University `sudhanchitgopkar[at]g.harvard.edu` # Lecture 1 ## Capital Markets Overview ### Types of Assets - Real assets (investments) - require an input of resources today and deliver output of resources later - Determine productive capacity of an economy - Financial assets - Claims to the output produced by real assets - *Capital markets are environments where financial assets are traded* ### Financial Asset Classification - Fixed income securities: - Promise to make fixed payments in the future - Bills (short-term, < 1 year), bonds (long-term, > 10 years) - Equities (stocks): - Claims to a share of the profit of a corporation - Derivative securities: - Make payments that depend on the prices of other financial assets - Futures, swaps, options ### Utility of Capital Markets - Shift the timing of consumption so it doesn't have to coincide with timing of income - Allocate risk (share amongst many or distribute to those who can bear it) - Allocate resources to the most productive real investment ## Functional Perspectives - Institutional details are abstracted away - Concepts remain the same across capitalist countries ## Basic Concepts of Finance - Arbitrage: What do we know about asset-prices by assuming there are no risk-free profit opportunities - Optimality: What is the best combination of risky financial assets given their returns and an individual investor's preferences? - Equilibrium: What is the nature of asset prices and allocations when all investors have optimized and markets clear? ## Asset Framework - Two periods, *now* and *future* - Finite number of states, $S$ of states may occurr in the future - some state $s \in \{1, \dots, S\}$ - Finite number of assets, $N$ available - some asset $n \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ - Assets cost money *now* and pay off in the *future* - Each asset has a payoff profile - $n$ pays off $X_{ns}$ in state $x$ - $n$ has payoff vector $[X_{n1}, \dots, X_{nS}]$ - Current price of $n$ is $p_n$ - $N$ assets are defined by an $N \times S$ matrix, $X$ $$\begin{array}{ccc}\\ X_{11} & \dots & X_{1S}\\ \vdots & \dots & \vdots\\ X_{N1} & \dots & X_{NS} \end{array}$$ - The price of $N$ assets can be stacked into an $N \times 1$ vector $$\begin{array}{c}\\ p_1\\ \vdots\\ p_N \end{array}$$ - We can form a portfolio by buying $w_n$ units of asset $n$ for each $n \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ - If we stack the number of shares into an $N \times 1$ vector, we get $$w = \begin{array}{c}\\ w_1\\ \vdots\\ w_N \end{array}$$ - And then we can find the payoff of our portfolio over time using $w^TX$ $$w^Tx = [\sum_{n = 1}^N w_nX_{n1} + \dots \sum_{n = 1}^N w_nX_{nS}]$$ - Finally, the price of our portfolio is $w^Tp = \sum_{n = 1}^N w_np_n$ ### Arrow-Debreu Securities - Portfolio that pays $1 in state $s$ and $0 in $s'$ - Called an Arrow-Debreu security - Has state price (current price) $q_s$ for state $s$ - With $S$ AD securities, one for each state, the payoff matrix is the identity matrix: $$X^{AD} = \begin{array}{cccc}\\ 1 & 0 & \dots & 0\\ 0 & 1 & \dots & 0\\ \vdots & \dots & \ddots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 & \dots & 1 \end{array}$$ - This market is **complete** because we can obtain any payoff profile by combining securities - This completeness extends to real markets: - We can contrive some combination of assets where $w^TX = [1, 0, \dots, 0]$ *as long as $N = S$ and assets are non-redundant (no asset payoff is a linear combination of others)* !!! Tip Thus, real markets are also complete! ## Arbitrage Theory *Arbitrage opportunity*: investment strategy that: - never requires a cash outflow now or in the future - Generates a cash inflow in 1+ states in the future (but not now) - Start by assuming no arbitrage opportunities - Has powerful implications for relative prices of assets ### Implications of No Arbitrage - Law of One Price: 2 assets or portfolios with the same payoffs in every future state must have the same price - Otherwise buy cheap one and sell the expensive one! - AD securities *must* have positive state prices - If ever negative, buy! cash inflow today and in 1 future state with no costs in any other state - If ever zero, buy it! Cash flow in 1+ future state with no costs at any other state - Generates particularly strong results when markets are complete - Tells us about relative pricing of asset, but not absolute - To understand absolute pricing, we need: - Optimization: the demand for consumption in state, $s$ - Equilibrium: adjustment of price, $q_s$ to ensure supply = demand
